Australians and New Zealanders have always thought of their slanguage as important to their cultural identity not surprisingly, given what went into the original dialect hot pot in the early years of English-speaking settlement.

The slang of convicts, sailors, whalers and gold-diggers mixed together with the emerging standard language this colloquial hotchpotch set old residents apart from new arrivals ('old chums' versus 'new chums', as they were known).

Author Edward Gibbon Wakefield wrote in 1829 of the flourishing of 'bad' language:

"The base language of English thieves is becoming the established language of the colony. Terms of slang and flash are used, as a matter of course, everywhere. No doubt they will be reckoned quite parliamentary, as soon as we obtain a parliament."

And so an informal culture with a slangy style of speech started what is now known as 'Australian English'.
